<Label>What Is PTIN</Label>

## What Is a <Accent>PTIN?</Accent>

A PTIN is a Preparer Tax Identification Number that the IRS requires for anyone who prepares federal tax returns for compensation. The format is the letter P followed by 8 digits. It identifies the individual preparer on every client return, not the preparer's business.

The IRS created the PTIN to track and regulate paid tax return preparers. Before the PTIN program, preparers wrote their own SSN on client returns. That exposed millions of SSNs and created fraud risk. The PTIN replaced the SSN on filed returns. A paid preparer now enters only the PTIN, which protects their SSN while still identifying them to the IRS.

A PTIN belongs to a person, not a firm. A CPA who works at a large accounting firm still holds a personal PTIN. The firm holds an EIN. The two numbers coexist on the same return: the firm's EIN identifies the business, and the preparer's PTIN identifies the individual who signed it.

| Number | Identifies | Issued To | Cost |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| PTIN | Paid tax preparer | Individual preparers | $19.75/year |
| EIN | Business entity | LLCs, corporations | $0 |
| SSN | US worker | US citizens and residents | $0 |
| ITIN | Non-SSN individual | Foreign individuals | $0 |

The PTIN is the only number in this table that carries an annual fee and an annual renewal requirement. An [EIN costs $0](/ein-cost/) and never expires. See the full [tax identification number overview](/tax-identification-number/) for how these numbers fit together.

## How Do You Get a <Accent>PTIN?</Accent>

You get a PTIN by applying online at irs.gov/ptin and paying the $19.75 fee. The IRS issues the PTIN immediately in most online cases. You need an SSN, your date of birth, and prior-year tax return information to verify your identity.

<Steps>
<Step step="1" title="Create an IRS PTIN Account">Go to irs.gov/ptin and set up an online account with your email and a password. This account is separate from any other IRS login you hold.</Step>
<Step step="2" title="Complete the Application">Enter your personal information, mailing address, and prior-year individual tax return details. The IRS uses these to confirm your identity before issuing the number.</Step>
<Step step="3" title="Pay the $19.75 Fee">Pay the annual fee by card or bank transfer. The fee applies to both first-time applications and yearly renewals.</Step>
<Step step="4" title="Receive Your PTIN">The IRS issues the PTIN immediately after the online application clears. Paper applications on Form W-12 take 4-6 weeks instead.</Step>
</Steps>

A US preparer applies online with an SSN. A preparer without an SSN must file paper Form W-12 with supporting identity documents, which extends processing to 4-6 weeks. Most non-residents never reach this step, because most non-residents are not paid US tax preparers. They are business owners who need an [EIN without an SSN](/ein-without-ssn/) instead.

</Section>

## Who Needs a <Accent>PTIN?</Accent>

A PTIN is required for anyone who prepares or assists in preparing federal tax returns for compensation. CPAs, enrolled agents, tax attorneys, and seasonal preparers all need one. Unpaid volunteers and people preparing only their own returns do not.

<NumberedCards>
<Card title="Paid Tax Preparers" index={1}>Any individual who prepares a federal tax return for a fee needs a PTIN. This includes independent preparers, storefront tax offices, and seasonal staff hired during filing season.</Card>
<Card title="CPAs and Enrolled Agents" index={2}>Licensed CPAs and enrolled agents who sign client returns hold a PTIN in addition to their professional credential. The PTIN sits on the return; the credential governs their practice rights before the IRS.</Card>
<Card title="Tax Attorneys" index={3}>Attorneys who prepare federal returns for clients for compensation need a PTIN, even when tax work is only part of their practice. The trigger is paid preparation, not the job title.</Card>
<Card title="Firm Staff Who Prepare Returns" index={4}>An employee who prepares returns for the firm's clients needs a personal PTIN. The firm uses its own EIN for billing and payroll, but each preparing employee holds an individual PTIN.</Card>
</NumberedCards>

<InfoBox title="Who Does NOT Need a PTIN">
<ul>
<li>People preparing only their own tax return</li>
<li>Family members helping without charging a fee</li>
<li>Unpaid VITA and TCE program volunteers</li>
<li>Business owners who need an EIN for their LLC, not a PTIN</li>
<li>Bookkeepers who do not prepare or sign federal returns</li>

## How Does a PTIN Differ From an <Accent>EIN?</Accent>

A PTIN identifies a paid tax preparer and costs $19.75 per year with annual renewal. An EIN identifies a business entity, costs $0, and never expires. The two numbers serve different parties on the same tax return and are never interchangeable.

Many business owners search for "PTIN" when they actually need an EIN. The confusion is understandable, because both are IRS-issued tax IDs. The distinction is simple: if you prepare tax returns for clients for money, you need a PTIN. If you own a US business and need a federal tax ID for it, you need an EIN.

| Factor | PTIN | EIN |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Purpose | Identifies a paid preparer | Identifies a business |
| Format | P + 8 digits | XX-XXXXXXX |
| Cost | $19.75 per year | $0 |
| Renewal | Annual, expires Dec 31 | Never expires |
| Apply method | Online at irs.gov/ptin | Form SS-4 |
| SSN required | Yes for online | No, passport works |
| Who applies | Individual preparers | Business owners |

A non-resident who forms a US LLC needs an EIN to open a US bank account, accept payments, and file US returns. That EIN application uses Form SS-4, not the PTIN system. See [what an EIN is](/what-is-ein/) and [how to get an EIN](/how-to-get-ein/) for the full process.

## Which Tax ID Does a <Accent>Non-Resident Business Owner Need?</Accent>

A non-resident business owner needs an EIN, not a PTIN. The EIN is the federal tax ID for a US LLC or corporation. You apply by faxing Form SS-4 to the IRS at 855-215-1627 using your passport number on Line 7b. No SSN or ITIN is needed.

The IRS online EIN tool requires an SSN, which blocks most non-residents. The fax method using Form SS-4 is the path for applicants without an SSN. ein.so prepares the SS-4, faxes it to the IRS, and delivers the EIN by email.

<Steps>
<Step step="1" title="Form Your US LLC">Register an LLC in a US state through a registered agent. This gives you a legal entity to attach the EIN to. You can do this entirely online from abroad.</Step>
<Step step="2" title="Complete Form SS-4">Enter your LLC name on Line 1, your foreign address on Line 4, your full name on Line 7a, and your passport number on Line 7b. No SSN is required.</Step>
<Step step="3" title="Submit by Fax">The SS-4 is faxed to the IRS at 855-215-1627. The IRS reviews and assigns the EIN to your business entity.</Step>
<Step step="4" title="Receive Your EIN">Standard processing takes 4-7 business days for $49. Express takes 2-3 business days for $97. The IRS returns the EIN assignment letter (CP 575).</Step>
</Steps>

The PTIN system has no role in this process. A business owner does not prepare returns for clients, so a PTIN does not apply. See [EIN for non-residents](/ein-for-non-residents/), the [SS-4 form guide](/ss4-form-guide/), and [EIN without an SSN](/ein-without-ssn/) for full detail.

## What Tax Compliance Follows a <Accent>US EIN?</Accent>

A foreign-owned single-member US LLC must file Form 5472 with a pro-forma Form 1120 each year after receiving its EIN. The penalty for not filing is $25,000. The EIN is the starting point for US tax compliance, not the end of it.

A PTIN carries its own compliance duty: a preparer must hold a valid, current PTIN on every paid return. A business owner's compliance is different and centers on the entity's annual filings.

<Card title="Form 5472 and Form 1120" index={1}>A foreign-owned single-member US LLC files [Form 5472](/form-5472-guide/) with a pro-forma Form 1120 each year. The penalty for non-filing is $25,000. This is an information return, not always a tax bill.</Card>
<Card title="BOI Report to FinCEN" index={2}>Most US LLCs report beneficial ownership to FinCEN. Review the [BOI filing rules](/boi-filing/) to stay compliant and avoid penalties.</Card>
<Card title="State Annual Reports" index={3}>Most states require an annual report and a state fee to keep the LLC in good standing. Confirm your state's deadline and fee with your registered agent.</Card>
