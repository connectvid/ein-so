<Capsule label="Short Answer">
**No. An EIN never expires.** Once the IRS assigns an EIN to your business, the number is permanent. It does not need renewal, it does not lapse from non-use, and the IRS never reissues it to another entity. An EIN issued decades ago is still valid today. The only number that expires is the ITIN, which is a different identifier entirely.
</Capsule>

<Section surface>

## Does an EIN <Accent>Expire?</Accent>

No. An EIN does not expire. The IRS states that an Employer Identification Number is a permanent federal taxpayer ID assigned to a single business entity for the life of that entity and beyond. There is no expiration date, no renewal cycle, and no fee to keep it active.

Once issued, your EIN — a 9-digit number in the format XX-XXXXXXX — stays attached to your business forever. Even if the business closes, the IRS retires the number rather than reassigning it. No other company will ever receive your EIN.

| Question | Answer |
| --- | --- |
| Does an EIN expire? | No, never |
| Does an EIN need renewal? | No |
| Is there a fee to maintain it? | No, the IRS charges $0 |
| Can the EIN be reused by another entity? | No, never |
| Does non-use cancel an EIN? | No |

See [what an EIN is](/what-is-ein/) for the full definition and how the number is structured.

</Section>

<Section>

## What Happens to an EIN <Accent>You Never Use?</Accent>

Nothing happens to an unused EIN. The number remains permanently assigned to your business, with no expiration clock running in the background. If you applied for an EIN and then never opened the business, the EIN simply sits on file at the IRS.

This surprises people who expect a "use it or lose it" rule. There is none for EINs. You can obtain an EIN in advance of launching, keep it dormant for years, and activate it later by using it on a tax return, a bank application, or a payroll filing. The IRS treats the number as valid the entire time.

An inactive EIN carries no penalty by itself. Penalties attach to missed filings — such as the $25,000 Form 5472 penalty for a foreign-owned LLC that fails to file — not to the EIN sitting unused.

</Section>

<Section surface>

## Can the IRS Deactivate <Accent>an EIN?</Accent>

The IRS can close the business account tied to an EIN, but it never cancels or reassigns the EIN itself. People confuse "closing the account" with "expiration." They are not the same.

If you close a business, you can send the IRS a written request to close the associated business account. The EIN stays permanently assigned to that entity. If you later need it again, you reactivate the account by using the EIN on a filing or by contacting the IRS — you do not apply for a new number.

| Action | Effect on the EIN |
| --- | --- |
| Close the business account | Account marked closed; EIN stays assigned |
| Stop using the EIN | EIN remains valid indefinitely |
| Dissolve the entity | EIN is retired, never reassigned |
| Reopen the business | Reactivate the same EIN |

For the formal process, see [EIN cancellation](/ein-cancellation/), which explains how account closure works without affecting the number's permanence.

</Section>

## Why Do People Think an EIN <Accent>Expires?</Accent>

People think an EIN expires mostly because they confuse it with the ITIN, which does expire. The two are different IRS identifiers with different rules.

- **The ITIN expires.** An Individual Taxpayer Identification Number lapses if it is not used on a federal tax return for 3 consecutive years, and it must be renewed. This is a real expiration rule — but it applies only to ITINs.
- **The EIN does not expire.** It has no use requirement and no renewal.
- **"Deactivation" is misread as expiration.** Closing a business account does not cancel the EIN.
- **Old confirmation letters cause doubt.** A CP 575 letter from years ago can make owners wonder if the number is stale. It is not.

See [EIN vs ITIN](/ein-vs-itin/) for a full comparison of the two numbers, including which one you actually need.

</Section>

## How Do You Recover <Accent>a Lost EIN?</Accent>

Recover a lost EIN by locating it, because the number was never lost from the IRS side and never expired. There are 4 reliable places to find it.

<Steps>
<Step step="1" title="Check Your CP 575 Letter">The IRS confirmation letter issued when your EIN was assigned, called the CP 575, shows the number. See [the EIN confirmation letter](/ein-confirmation-letter/) guide for what it looks like.</Step>
<Step step="2" title="Look at a Prior Tax Return or Bank Record">Your EIN appears on every federal return you have filed and on your business bank account paperwork. Pull either document to read the number.</Step>
<Step step="3" title="Request a Letter 147C">If no document survives, ask the IRS for a [Letter 147C](/irs-letter-147c/), which restates your existing EIN. The IRS confirms the same number — it does not issue a new one.</Step>
</Steps>

Because an EIN never expires, recovery always means finding the original number, not applying again. See [lost EIN number](/lost-ein-number/) for every recovery path.
