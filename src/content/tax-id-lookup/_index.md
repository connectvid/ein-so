A Tax ID number, also called a Taxpayer Identification Number (TIN), is a 9-digit number the IRS uses to track a person or business for tax purposes. The three main types are the Employer Identification Number (EIN) for businesses, the Social Security Number (SSN) for individuals, and the Individual Taxpayer Identification Number (ITIN) for individuals without an SSN. Looking up a Tax ID depends entirely on which type you need and who it belongs to. Nonprofit and public company EINs are public records. Private company EINs, SSNs, and ITINs are confidential. This guide maps every legitimate lookup method, explains the legal limits, and shows non-residents how to obtain their own EIN.

| TIN Type | Issued To | Public Lookup? | Lookup Method |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| EIN (nonprofit) | Tax-exempt orgs | Yes | IRS Tax Exempt Search |
| EIN (public company) | SEC filers | Yes | SEC EDGAR |
| EIN (private company) | Private businesses | No | W-9 request |
| SSN | US individuals | No | Confidential |
| ITIN | Individuals (no SSN) | No | Confidential |
</Section>

## What Are the Methods to Look Up a <Accent>Tax ID Number?</Accent>

Four legitimate methods look up a Tax ID number: the IRS Tax Exempt Organization Search for nonprofits, SEC EDGAR for public companies, state Secretary of State databases for registered businesses, and a W-9 request for any private company. Each method targets a different TIN type and source.

No single tool returns every Tax ID. The IRS does not publish a master directory. You pick the method that matches the entity you are researching. The table below ranks each method by cost and what it returns.

| Method | Finds | Source | Cost |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| IRS Tax Exempt Search | Nonprofit EINs | irs.gov | Free |
| SEC EDGAR | Public company EINs | sec.gov | Free |
| State SOS database | Registered business records | State website | Free |
| W-9 request | Any business EIN | The company | Free |
| IRS phone (own EIN only) | Your own EIN | 800-829-4933 | Free |

For EIN-specific research, see [EIN lookup](/ein-lookup/) and [EIN lookup by name](/ein-lookup-by-name/). To verify a number you already have, see [EIN verification](/ein-verification/).

## How Do You Look Up a Nonprofit's Tax ID <Accent>Number?</Accent>

Look up a nonprofit's Tax ID number using the IRS Tax Exempt Organization Search at irs.gov, which returns the EIN, legal name, and tax-exempt status for free. Nonprofit EINs are public because tax-exempt organizations file disclosure returns the public can read.

The IRS tool searches registered 501(c)(3) and other tax-exempt entities. You enter the organization name, city, or state and the tool returns matching records. Two independent databases also publish this data from filed Form 990 returns.

<NumberedCards>
<Card title="IRS Tax Exempt Organization Search" index={1}>Go to irs.gov and open Tax Exempt Organization Search. Enter the nonprofit name. The result shows the EIN, exempt status, and last filing. This is the authoritative source and costs $0.</Card>
<Card title="ProPublica Nonprofit Explorer" index={2}>ProPublica publishes EINs and full Form 990 filings for hundreds of thousands of nonprofits. Search by name to find the EIN plus revenue, executive pay, and grants. The data comes from public IRS releases.</Card>
<Card title="GuideStar by Candid" index={3}>GuideStar aggregates nonprofit profiles, including EINs, mission statements, and financials. A free account unlocks basic records. Larger data exports require a paid plan.</Card>
<Card title="The Form 990 Itself" index={4}>Every nonprofit Form 990 lists the EIN on the first page. If you have a copy of the return, the EIN is printed near the organization name in the header block.</Card>
</NumberedCards>

## How Do You Find a Public Company's Tax ID <Accent>Number?</Accent>

Find a public company's Tax ID number using SEC EDGAR at sec.gov, which is free. Public companies list their EIN on the cover page of SEC filings such as the 10-K annual report and the 10-Q quarterly report, labeled "IRS Employer Identification Number."

SEC-reporting companies must disclose their EIN in filings. Private companies and most foreign companies do not file with the SEC, so EDGAR will not have them. Follow these steps to find the number.

<Steps>
<Step step="1" title="Open SEC EDGAR">Go to sec.gov and open the EDGAR full-text search. EDGAR holds filings from every US public company.</Step>
<Step step="2" title="Search the Company Name">Enter the exact public company name. EDGAR returns the company's filing history and its central index key (CIK).</Step>
<Step step="3" title="Open a Recent 10-K or 10-Q">Open the most recent annual or quarterly report. The cover page lists the company name, state of incorporation, and IRS Employer Identification Number.</Step>
<Step step="4" title="Read the EIN">The EIN appears in XX-XXXXXXX format near the top of the filing. Copy it directly from the official document.</Step>
</Steps>

EDGAR works only for SEC filers. For a private company, skip EDGAR and use the W-9 method described below.

## How Do You Get a Private Company's Tax ID <Accent>Number?</Accent>

Get a private company's Tax ID number by requesting IRS Form W-9 directly from the company, which lists its EIN. No public IRS search returns private company EINs. The W-9 request is the standard business method when you must report payments on a 1099 form.

Private company EINs stay confidential under IRC Section 6103. The company itself is the source. Use one of these legitimate routes.

| Source | Where to Look | When It Works |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Form W-9 | Request from the company | You pay the company and file 1099s |
| Past 1099-NEC / 1099-MISC | Your own records | The company already paid you or you paid them |
| Signed contract or invoice | Document header or signature block | The EIN was disclosed in writing |
| State SOS database | State business registry | The state publishes entity details |
| Business credit report | Dun & Bradstreet, paid services | You need due-diligence data |

A vendor or contractor must give you a completed W-9 when you request it for tax reporting. The form shows the legal name and the EIN on the same page. See [EIN lookup IRS](/ein-lookup-irs/) for what the IRS does and does not publish.

## How Do You Recover Your Own Tax ID <Accent>Number?</Accent>

Recover your own Tax ID number from your IRS CP 575 assignment letter, a prior business tax return, a bank application, or a payroll document. If none are available, call the IRS Business and Specialty Tax Line at 800-829-4933 to verify your own EIN after an identity check.

The IRS confirms your own EIN because you are authorized for that entity. It will not read out a number for a company you do not control. For an ITIN you cannot find, call 800-829-1040.

<NumberedCards>
<Card title="Check the CP 575 Letter" index={1}>The IRS mailed or faxed a CP 575 confirmation when it assigned your EIN. The 9-digit number appears at the top. This is the original record.</Card>
<Card title="Check Prior Tax Filings" index={2}>Your EIN appears on every business tax return you filed, including Form 1120, Form 1065, and Form 5472. Pull a prior-year copy to read it.</Card>
<Card title="Check Bank and Payroll Records" index={3}>You supplied your EIN when you opened a business bank account or set up payroll. The number sits in those onboarding documents.</Card>
<Card title="Call the IRS" index={4}>Call 800-829-4933, 7am to 7pm local time. After verifying you are the responsible party, the agent reads your EIN. This is free.</Card>
</NumberedCards>

## Why Is Tax ID Number Lookup <Accent>So Limited?</Accent>

Tax ID number lookup is limited because IRC Section 6103 classifies tax identification numbers as confidential federal tax information. The IRS publishes nonprofit and public company EINs only because those entities file public disclosure returns. Private EINs, SSNs, and ITINs are never published.

This privacy rule prevents identity theft and tax fraud. A leaked EIN can be used to open fraudulent accounts or file false returns. The same logic blocks public SSN and ITIN lookups entirely.

<Alert type="warning" title="Tax IDs you cannot look up publicly">
<ol>
<li>Private company EINs are confidential. Request a W-9 instead.</li>
<li>Individual SSNs have no public lookup. The Social Security Administration does not publish them.</li>
<li>Individual ITINs are confidential. Only the holder and the IRS know them.</li>
<li>You cannot use the IRS to find a third party's number. The agency confirms only your own.</li>
</ol>
</Alert>

Be cautious with third-party sites that promise a "universal tax ID lookup." Many sell repackaged public data or charge for information that government tools provide for free. See [tax identification number](/tax-identification-number/) for the full definition of each TIN type.
