## What Are the Ways to Look Up an EIN by <Accent>Company Name?</Accent>

Five methods exist to look up an EIN by company name: the IRS Tax Exempt Organization Search, SEC EDGAR, state Secretary of State databases, a direct IRS Form W-9 request, and paid commercial data services. The first four are free. Only nonprofits and public companies appear in public databases.

Each method works for a different category of organization. Match the method to the entity type, and use the exact legal name rather than the brand or DBA name. A partial name returns no match.

| Method | Works For | Searches By | Cost |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| IRS Tax Exempt Search | Registered nonprofits | Name, city, state | Free |
| SEC EDGAR | Public companies | Name or ticker | Free |
| State Secretary of State | Registered businesses | Legal entity name | Free |
| IRS Form W-9 request | Any business you transact with | Direct request | Free |
| Commercial services | Any (limited coverage) | Name | Paid |

The W-9 request is the only method that reliably reaches a private company. The IRS does not publish private EINs, so a direct request is the standard business practice. The remaining sections explain each method in detail.

## How Do I Find a Nonprofit's EIN by <Accent>Name?</Accent>

Find a nonprofit's EIN using the free IRS Tax Exempt Organization Search at apps.irs.gov/app/eos. Enter the organization's name, then filter by city and state. The results list the EIN, the legal name, the location, and the federal tax-exempt status. This tool covers organizations that the IRS recognizes as tax-exempt.

<Steps>
<Step step="1" title="Open the IRS Tool">Go to apps.irs.gov/app/eos in any browser. This is the official IRS Tax Exempt Organization Search. It is free and requires no account or login.</Step>
<Step step="2" title="Enter the Organization Name">Type the exact legal name of the nonprofit. If you only know a partial name, enter the most distinctive word and review the results list.</Step>
<Step step="3" title="Filter by City and State">Use the city and state filters to narrow large result sets. Many nonprofits share similar names, so location separates them.</Step>
<Step step="4" title="Read the Result">Each match shows the EIN, the legal name, the city and state, and the tax-exempt status. The 9-digit EIN appears in the XX-XXXXXXX format.</Step>
</Steps>

Nonprofits also file IRS Form 990 each year, which is a public document. Third-party sites such as ProPublica Nonprofit Explorer and Candid GuideStar index Form 990 data and display EINs. These sites pull from the same public filings, so they confirm what the IRS tool returns.
</Section>

## How Do I Find a Public Company's EIN by <Accent>Name?</Accent>

Find a public company's EIN using SEC EDGAR at sec.gov/edgar, the free full-text search system from the Securities and Exchange Commission. Search the company name or ticker symbol, open a recent filing such as a 10-K, and read the EIN on the cover page labeled "I.R.S. Employer Identification No."

Public companies must disclose their EIN in SEC filings by law. The cover page of an annual report (Form 10-K) and a quarterly report (Form 10-Q) lists the EIN near the company name and state of incorporation. EDGAR holds decades of filings, so even delisted companies often remain searchable.

| Filing | Where the EIN Appears | Filed |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Form 10-K | Cover page header | Annually |
| Form 10-Q | Cover page header | Quarterly |
| Form 8-K | Cover page header | On material events |
| Form S-1 | Registration cover page | Before IPO |

To open a filing, search the company in EDGAR, click the company's filing history, then open the most recent 10-K. The EIN sits beside the phrase "I.R.S. Employer Identification No." on the first page. This method works only for companies that file with the SEC.

## How Do I Get a Private Company's EIN by <Accent>Name?</Accent>

Get a private company's EIN by requesting IRS Form W-9 directly from the business, because no public database lists private EINs. A business you pay $600 or more in a year for services must provide a completed W-9 showing its legal name and EIN. Collect the W-9 before you make the first payment.

The IRS treats a private company's EIN as confidential business information. There is no public IRS directory of private EINs, and no free tool returns them by name. This is by design, not an oversight. Your reliable options are limited.

<NumberedCards>
<Card title="Request IRS Form W-9" index={1}>This is the standard method. A business that receives $600 or more from you in a year for services must complete Form W-9 with its legal name and EIN. You need this to file Form 1099-NEC. Ask for it before the first payment.</Card>
<Card title="Check State Databases" index={2}>Some state Secretary of State business registries list registered entities and, in certain states, an associated tax ID. Coverage and the fields shown vary widely by state. Search the exact legal entity name.</Card>
<Card title="Search Public Documents" index={3}>A private company sometimes publishes its EIN in a public contract, a grant filing, or a press release. A targeted Google search occasionally surfaces this. The result is unreliable and should not replace a W-9.</Card>
<Card title="Use Commercial Data Services" index={4}>Dun and Bradstreet and LexisNexis sell business identity data. Coverage is partial and the service is paid. These tools target compliance and credit teams, not casual lookups.</Card>
</NumberedCards>

## How Do I Look Up My Own EIN if I <Accent>Lost It?</Accent>

Look up your own lost EIN on your IRS confirmation letter (CP 575), a prior business tax return, an old bank account application, or a past payroll filing. If no record survives, call the IRS Business and Specialty Tax Line at 800-829-4933 during US business hours to retrieve it.

Your EIN does not change once issued, so any old document that used it remains a valid source. Check these places in order before calling the IRS.

<NumberedCards>
<Card title="CP 575 Confirmation Letter" index={1}>The IRS mails or faxes a CP 575 letter when it assigns your EIN. This letter is the primary source. Search your email and physical files for it. If you applied through ein.so, the EIN arrives by email.</Card>
<Card title="Prior Tax Return" index={2}>Any federal business return your company filed lists the EIN on the first page. A foreign-owned single-member LLC, for example, reports its EIN on the [Form 5472](/form-5472-guide/) package.</Card>
<Card title="Bank and Payment Records" index={3}>Your US business bank account application captured the EIN. Stripe, PayPal, and payroll providers also store it. Check the account profile or onboarding documents.</Card>
<Card title="IRS Phone Line" index={4}>Call 800-829-4933 if no document survives. The IRS agent verifies your identity as the responsible party, then reads back the EIN. International callers reach this line during US business hours.</Card>
</NumberedCards>

If you discover that your business never had an EIN, you need to apply for one. Non-residents without an SSN use the fax method described below, which ein.so handles end to end.

## What Do I Do After I Have an <Accent>EIN?</Accent>

After you have an EIN, open a US business bank account, connect payment processors, and meet your annual federal filing duties. A foreign-owned single-member US LLC files [Form 5472](/form-5472-guide/) plus a pro-forma Form 1120 each year. The penalty for not filing Form 5472 is $25,000.

The EIN is the starting point, not the finish line. Use it to access US banking and stay compliant.
