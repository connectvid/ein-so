Misplacing the CP 575 confirmation letter does not cancel the number. You recover the same EIN — you do not apply for a new one. This guide covers the 3 recovery methods, who is allowed to request the number, how Letter 147C works, and how to prevent losing it again. ## What Are the 3 Ways to Recover a <Accent>Lost EIN?</Accent>

Recover a lost EIN by calling the IRS, checking existing documents, or requesting Letter 147C. Each method returns the same permanent 9-digit number. The phone and Letter 147C methods require you to be the responsible party. Document checks require no IRS contact.

<NumberedCards>
<Card title="Call the IRS Business Line" index={1}>Call 800-829-4933 inside the US or 267-941-1099 from abroad. The IRS verifies your identity, then reads your EIN aloud during the call. You must be the responsible party or hold Power of Attorney on Form 2848. This is the fastest method for an immediate answer.</Card>
<Card title="Check Existing Documents" index={2}>Your EIN appears on your CP 575 confirmation letter, every prior tax return, your business bank account paperwork, and W-2 or 1099 forms you issued. This method needs no IRS contact and works in minutes if your records are organized.</Card>
<Card title="Request Letter 147C" index={3}>Call 800-829-4933 and ask for an EIN verification letter, called Letter 147C. The IRS faxes it within 24-48 hours or mails it within 10 business days. Banks and payment processors accept Letter 147C as proof of your EIN.</Card>
</NumberedCards>

### Where Your EIN Already Appears

Before calling the IRS, scan these documents. Your EIN is on all of them:

- CP 575 — the original EIN confirmation letter from the IRS
- Prior tax returns — Form 1120, Form 1065, or Schedule C
- Form 5472 — filed annually by foreign-owned single-member LLCs
- Business bank account opening documents
- W-2 and 1099 forms your business issued
- State business registration and annual report filings
- IRS correspondence and notices

## How Do You Recover a Lost EIN by <Accent>Calling the IRS?</Accent>

Call the IRS Business and Specialty Tax Line at 800-829-4933 inside the US, or 267-941-1099 from outside the US. The agent verifies your identity as the responsible party, then provides your EIN verbally. The call is free on the domestic line.

<Steps>
<Step step="1" title="Confirm You Are the Responsible Party">The IRS releases an EIN only to the person listed as the responsible party on the original Form SS-4. If someone else applied, they must make the call or grant you Form 2848 Power of Attorney first.</Step>
<Step step="2" title="Call During Operating Hours">Dial 800-829-4933 Monday to Friday, 7am to 7pm Eastern Time. International callers dial 267-941-1099, which is not toll-free. Have your entity legal name, address, and your own identification ready.</Step>
<Step step="3" title="Pass Identity Verification">The agent asks security questions to confirm you are authorized. Be prepared to state the exact legal name of the entity and the mailing address on file with the IRS.</Step>
<Step step="4" title="Receive Your EIN Verbally">The agent reads your 9-digit EIN aloud. Write it down and store it in two places. Ask for Letter 147C in the same call if you need written proof for a bank.</Step>
</Steps>

Non-residents face one extra hurdle: the 267-941-1099 line operates on US Eastern Time, so callers in Europe, Asia, and Australia call at inconvenient hours and pay international call rates. ## What Is Letter 147C and How Do You <Accent>Request It?</Accent>

Letter 147C is the official IRS EIN verification letter. It restates your EIN, legal entity name, and address on IRS letterhead. Request it by calling 800-829-4933 as the responsible party. The IRS faxes it within 24-48 hours or mails it within 10 business days.

Letter 147C exists because the IRS issues the CP 575 confirmation letter only once. The IRS does not reprint CP 575. When you lose the original, Letter 147C becomes the replacement document. Banks, Stripe, PayPal, and state agencies accept Letter 147C as proof of your EIN.

| Document | When Issued | Reissued? | Use |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| CP 575 | Once, when EIN is first assigned | No | Original proof of EIN |
| Letter 147C | On request, anytime after | Yes | Replacement proof of EIN |

To request Letter 147C, call 800-829-4933, confirm your identity, and ask for an "EIN verification letter." Choose fax for the 24-48 hour turnaround, or mail for delivery within 10 business days. A fresh EIN holder who wants the original document should read [EIN confirmation letter](/ein-confirmation-letter/).

## Who Is Allowed to Recover an <Accent>EIN?</Accent>

Only the responsible party listed on Form SS-4 can recover an EIN directly from the IRS. A third party needs Power of Attorney on Form 2848 or Third Party Designee authorization on Form 8821. The IRS verifies identity before releasing any EIN information.

This rule protects your entity from fraud. An accountant, lawyer, or business partner who is not the responsible party cannot phone the IRS and collect your EIN without written authorization on file.

<InfoBox title="Authorization Routes to Recover an EIN">
<ul>
<li><strong>Responsible party</strong> — the individual named on Form SS-4 Line 7a recovers the EIN directly by phone.</li>
<li><strong>Form 2848 (Power of Attorney)</strong> — grants a representative authority to act on the entity's behalf, including EIN recovery.</li>
<li><strong>Form 8821 (Tax Information Authorization)</strong> — lets a designee receive tax information, including EIN confirmation.</li>
<li><strong>Successor responsible party</strong> — update the IRS using Form 8822-B within 60 days of any change.</li>
</ul>
</InfoBox>

If your entity's responsible party has changed, file Form 8822-B to update the IRS before attempting recovery. The IRS matches the caller against its current records. A mismatch blocks the EIN release.

## Should You Recover a Lost EIN or Apply for a <Accent>New One?</Accent>

Recover your existing EIN — do not apply for a new one. Applying for a second EIN creates duplicate tax records and IRS confusion. A new EIN is correct only when you form a new entity or change your business structure, such as incorporating a sole proprietorship.

A lost EIN is a records problem, not a number problem. The number still exists. Compare the two situations before you act:

| Situation | Correct Action | Why |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Lost the CP 575 paperwork | Recover existing EIN | The EIN is permanent and unchanged |
| Forgot the number | Recover via phone or Letter 147C | No new EIN needed |
| Formed a brand-new entity | Apply for a new EIN | Each entity needs its own EIN |
| Changed structure (sole prop to corp) | Apply for a new EIN | Structure change requires a new number |
| Bought an existing business | Apply for a new EIN | New ownership needs a new number |

Filing a duplicate EIN forces you to later contact the IRS to close one number, which delays banking and tax filing. ## How Do You Prevent Losing Your EIN <Accent>Again?</Accent>

Prevent future EIN loss by saving the CP 575 digitally, recording the number in multiple systems, and requesting a backup Letter 147C. Your EIN is permanent, so a single secure record protects you for the life of the entity.

<Steps>
<Step step="1" title="Save CP 575 Digitally">Scan the original CP 575 letter to cloud storage and a password manager. The IRS never reissues CP 575, so a digital copy is your only durable backup of the original document.</Step>
<Step step="2" title="Record the EIN in Multiple Places">Store the 9-digit number in your accounting software, your password manager, and your bank profile. Three independent records mean one lost file never costs you the number.</Step>
<Step step="3" title="Request a Backup Letter 147C">Call 800-829-4933 once and request Letter 147C as a spare verification document. Banks and processors accept it whenever a vendor or platform demands written proof.</Step>
<Step step="4" title="Keep Tax Returns Accessible">Your EIN appears on every tax return. Foreign-owned single-member LLCs file Form 5472 each year, so your annual filing doubles as a permanent EIN record.</Step>
</Steps>

<Alert type="warning" title="Foreign-owned LLCs: protect your EIN for annual filing">
<ol>
<li>A foreign-owned single-member US LLC files Form 5472 plus a pro-forma Form 1120 every year.</li>
<li>The penalty for failing to file Form 5472 is $25,000.</li>
<li>You need your EIN on each annual filing, so losing it disrupts compliance.</li>
<li>Review the [Form 5472 guide](/form-5472-guide/) and confirm your filing duties with a CPA.</li>
</ol>
