## Why Does Every Nonprofit <Accent>Need an EIN?</Accent>

Every nonprofit needs an EIN because the IRS treats it as a separate legal entity with its own reporting obligations. A nonprofit cannot apply for tax-exempt status, file Form 990, open a bank account, or receive grants without one. The EIN is the foundation of the organization's federal identity.

More than 1.5 million tax-exempt organizations are registered with the IRS, and every one holds an EIN. A nonprofit pays $0 in federal income tax once the IRS grants exempt status. It still carries 5 core reporting duties that require an EIN.

### 5 Core EIN Requirements for Nonprofits

<NumberedCards>
<Card title="501(c)(3) Application" index={1}>Form 1023 and Form 1023-EZ both require your EIN on line 1. The IRS will not process your tax-exempt application without it. Get your EIN first, then file for tax-exempt status. See [how to get an EIN](/how-to-get-ein/) for the full sequence.</Card>
<Card title="Form 990 Annual Filing" index={2}>Most tax-exempt organizations file Form 990, 990-EZ, or 990-N each year. Your EIN identifies the organization on these returns. Missing Form 990 for 3 consecutive years triggers automatic revocation of tax-exempt status.</Card>
<Card title="Bank Accounts and Donations" index={3}>Banks require an EIN to open a nonprofit account. Donors who give $250 or more need a written acknowledgment. Grant-making foundations verify your EIN against the IRS exempt organizations database before funding programs.</Card>
<Card title="Payroll and Employment" index={4}>A nonprofit with paid staff uses the EIN to withhold income tax, Social Security, and Medicare. The EIN appears on W-2 forms, Form 941 (quarterly payroll returns), and Form 940 (FUTA returns).</Card>
<Card title="State Registrations" index={5}>Most states require charitable solicitation registration before fundraising. These state filings reference your federal EIN. State tax exemption applications also reference the federal EIN.</Card>
</NumberedCards>

## Where Does the EIN Fit in the <Accent>501(c)(3) Application Process?</Accent>

The EIN is step 2 of 5 in nonprofit formation, and it takes minutes to request. The full 501(c)(3) determination takes 3-6 months for Form 1023, so the EIN should never be the bottleneck. Request the EIN immediately after you incorporate.

## What Mistakes Do Nonprofits Make <Accent>on EIN Applications?</Accent>

4 errors cause most nonprofit EIN application delays: wrong entity type, organization-as-responsible-party, name mismatch, and applying too late. Each one creates record problems that surface later when you file Form 1023 or open a bank account.

### Error #1: Wrong Entity Type Selection

Selecting "Corporation" instead of "Other nonprofit organization" sets up your IRS account incorrectly. Many nonprofits incorporate at the state level, but the IRS classifies nonprofit entities differently from for-profit corporations. The wrong type causes a record mismatch when you file Form 1023, and correcting it requires a written request to the IRS that adds weeks.

### Error #2: Using the Organization as the Responsible Party

The responsible party must be an individual person, such as the board president or executive director. Entering the organization's name in the responsible party field produces a rejected application. Enter the officer's name on line 7a and their ID number on line 7b.

### Error #3: Mismatching the Legal Name

The name on the EIN application must match the articles of incorporation exactly. Abbreviations, punctuation changes, or a missing "Inc." cause problems when you file for tax-exempt status or open a bank account. Copy the legal name character for character.

### Error #4: Applying Too Late

Some founders delay the EIN, assuming tax-exempt status comes first. The opposite is true: you need the EIN before you apply for tax-exempt status. Request the EIN immediately after incorporating.
